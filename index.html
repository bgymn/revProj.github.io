An issue with no end in sight..

What is an Urban Heat Island or "UHI"?

A heat island is an area in which the temperature is higher than surrounding areas. An urban heat island or UHI is an urban area where temperatures are significantly higher than surrounding rural areas as a result of human activities (Letcher, 2022). Heat is retained more when large areas of natural land are being covered by impervious surfaces like buildings roofs, pavement, or parking lots. This is also the result of the energy created from people and motorized vehicles. Hence the term, urban. Green spaces, or lack thereof, correlate with UHIs. This causes countless problems, especially in big cities but in smaller growing cities as well. With the increasing population in Colorado; Colorado Springs specifically, the city is at risk for such potential harms from UHIs. The impact here can not be taken more seriously.

Impacts..

UHIs affect energy costs at the minimum, pollution in the air, and can lead to health concerns. The city’s population can really take a hit; older adults, infants, pregnant women, athletes, outdoor workers including first responders like firefighters, low income neighborhoods, homeless people, the list goes on. UHI’s often cause rolling blackouts as well. When all the people in the city are using air conditioning and fans, it can be extraneous on a power grid or on utility companies, and the power goes out. This is a simple inconvenience to some but concerning for the more and more people who work from home, and especially for the people who rely on their electricity for health reasons; being on oxygen, for example. There’s a clear impact on society, and even more so if nothing is done.

Possibilities towards a better, sustainable future.

What are some solutions to such a widespread and growing issue?

The solutions to Urban Heat Islands (UHI) that can be applied in Colorado Springs are widely varied. Some have a very high level of success but are also financially demanding. Others are effective but would replace systems that are useful for gathering heat in the winter months. These options are still worth discussing alongside the solutions that Students for Sustainable Infrastructure (SSI) are fighting to achieve. This is because, while we advocate options that are accessible to low income households, there are many options that high income homes can use to combat UHIs. Research shows that UHIs affect low income areas disproportionately, but the problem is not exclusive to low-income neighborhoods. There are areas in and around Colorado Springs, such as Falcon, that are not low-income but also qualify as a UHI. These areas can benefit from solutions that are not practical in low-income areas.

Green Roofing

Green Roofing is a method that can be used to reduce UHIs by replacing the roof material with vegetation. This not only eliminates the heat-trapping roof area but also adds vegetation to reduce residual heat even more. There are many manufacturers of Green Roofing and it can be a very effective, albeit costly, option. This solution is not always cost effective due to the water needed in order to maintain the living vegetation. While Colorado Springs is an area that must conserve water, certain manufacturers do have low consumption options, and other states could benefit greatly from Living Roofs due to their low water costs and higher rainfall.

Increase Public Green Space

Everyone loves living in a green area. In addition to aesthetics, green landscapes can also help combat the UHI effect. At SSI, we are proposing to the Colorado Springs City Zoning Board that any apartment complex being built must incorporate a minimum ratio of 1 square foot “green/blue space” (GBS) to every 3 square feet of “non-green space” (NGS) when viewed from above. We are also proposing that shopping complexes meet a 1 to 5 ratio of GBS to NGS. GBS can include any surface that is covered by water, grass, trees, shrubs or similar vegetation. NGS is any surface area that is concrete, asphalt, roofing, or metal parking coverage. This would effectively limit the contribution of apartment and shopping complexes to the UHI. While these actions will help with future building projects, we are also proposing that any businesses, companies or corporations that own or lease any more than 1 acre of land meet the same 1 to 5 ratio within 1 year to continue to operate. This is a useful limitation to ensure that the policy would include nearly all grocery stores, super stores (Walmart, Costco, King Soopers), churches, and other large users of land, but not local businesses. It is impossible, in practical application, that most small business owners could meet such a requirement. Out of all we are doing at SSI, getting these proposals put into practice would make the largest difference in the development of UHIs. We could, in theory, solve the majority of the issue within a few short years and become an example to other cities that struggle greatly to mitigate the rising temperatures.

Community "Green-Up" Projects

At SSI we also believe in making gradual changes on a personal level. While the proposals would achieve the most sweeping changes, there is much that can be done to help each person mitigate UHI effects. Our community Green-Up programs bring people into the low-income neighborhoods most impacted by the heat to increase greenery and energy conservation. We have partnered with local companies and lobbied for city and state rebate programs to get homeowners new greenery and more efficient doors and windows. With this program, homeowners will face no costs. We make sure to balance their energy savings with the water that they will need to use on the greenery and all materials and installation are covered through charitable donations and credits we receive from our partners. We use native drought resistant shrubs, trees and ground cover such as lavender and prairie grass that only need to be watered in the driest periods of extreme heat. No thirsty lawns here.
